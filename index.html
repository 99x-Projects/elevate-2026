✦Why ELEVATE exists

One evening, 25+ teams, one purpose — and it starts with what our customers are really asking for.

The ground we stood on

We built software one sprint at a time.

For twenty years, software ran on effort — backlogs, sprints, tickets. Customers counted on our hours and trusted us to spend them well. Effort was the measure of value.

Capacity · Tasks · Tickets · Hours
The customer asked
"How many people can you give us?"
The ground is moving

Effort is no longer scarce — judgment is.

Agents now write a growing share of the world's code — implementation is becoming something you direct, not something you type. So customers stop asking for people who work on tasks, and start looking for a partner who takes responsibility for the product: the outcome, measured, owned.

~Half of new code is AI-assisted · Contracts moving from hours to outcomes · Judgment at the gate
The customer now asks
"Can you own the outcome?"
Our answer

Elevate project performance.

"Incorporate AI into the software development lifecycle in a way that elevates project performance." That's the 99x NorthStar: Higher PaceBetter QualityExtended ValueA Team That Thrives

People

Engineers who own outcomes — understand the domain, hold the gate, answer for the number.

Process

An AI-first lifecycle (AI-DLC) — development re-engineered so agents deliver and humans decide.

Platform

Governed agents at work — deployed fast, guardrails and compliance built in, like the Agent Control Plane we're building.

ELEVATE is your opportunity to step into all three: give an agent one real job on one real project, hold the human gate, and prove the difference with a measured number. The strongest of the night go to the 99x expert panel — then to customers as real offerings.

1Find your level — the 5 waves of AI tooling

We've moved from copy/paste prompts to open enterprise platforms in five short years. Waves 1–3 made one developer faster — and the promise of a fully automated SDLC broke. The real elevation is waves 4–5: lifecycle automation by a hybrid team. Build one level up from where you work today: a wave-4 build — an agent that owns a lifecycle stage, with a human at the gate.

2Find your surface — the four NorthStar pillars

How to find the pain: walk your project stage by stage — discovery · construction · operation · adoption — and ask "what recurs, what does it cost, who feels it?" The pain you pick is scorecard step 1, and the pillar it maps to is step 2 — judges score the mapping before they ever look at your agent.

3Pick your agent play

You don't have to invent the pattern — agents already earn their keep at every lifecycle stage, and the index at the end of this tab lists the proven plays. Steal one, aim it at your pain, and put a human decision gate in front of production — that gate is scorecard step 4.

4One team, one metric

The judges score the elevation, not the agent — and an elevation is a number that moved. A number can only move against a baseline taken before the agent touched anything. That's what the two weeks between ramp-up and event day are for: they are your measurement window, not just build time.

Pick one metric. Baseline it in week one. Drive it in week two. Bring both numbers to your table.

Your metric belongs to the pillar you declared Higher PaceBetter QualityExtended ValueA Team That Thrives — the menu below gives you proven, modern metric families for each. Pick what your team can honestly measure in two weeks — SPACE for how the team works, HEART for what your users feel, DORA where you own the pipeline. Sources at the end of this tab.

📐The metric menu — grouped to the NorthStar

One driver metric per team, from your declared pillar's column. Arrows show the direction that counts: ↓ drive it down, ↑ drive it up.

Higher Pace

SPACE efficiency & flow · DORA throughput
  • Lead time for a real task ↓ — time it before vs. with the agent, 3–6 real runs
  • Task cycle time (idea → in production) ↓
  • Deployment frequency ↑
  • Review / hand-off wait time ↓
  • Uninterrupted focus blocks per week ↑ (SPACE)
  • Perceived rate of delivery ↑ (DX Core 4 pulse)
Two-week trick: don't wait for org-level stats — stopwatch the actual slow task this week, then again with the agent next week.

Better Quality

DORA stability · rework thinking
  • Rework rate ↓ — DORA's newest key metric
  • Change failure rate ↓
  • Defects caught at the gate ↑ / escaped defects ↓
  • Failed-deployment recovery time ↓
  • Flaky-test rate ↓
Two-week trick: don't chase percentages that need months of data — count what actually happened: "our gate caught 9 of the 11 defects before they reached the merge."

Extended Value

Google HEART · customer KPIs · AI resolution
  • The KPI your customer already tracks ↑
  • Task success on the pilot ↑ — did the user finish the job? (HEART)
  • Ticket first-response / resolution time ↓
  • Resolution rate ↑ — share handled end-to-end by the agent, human at the gate
  • % effort moved from toil to billable work ↑
  • Cost per run / per transaction ↓
Two-week trick: pick the number the customer would put on their own dashboard — that's the one that survives the judges' Q&A.

A Team That Thrives

SPACE · DevEx · DX Core 4 effectiveness
  • Time reclaimed from toil, per person per week ↑
  • 3-question team pulse (friction · flow · cognitive load) ↑ — the questions are in the index
  • Interrupt & on-call load ↓
  • "Would you keep it on Monday?" ↑
Two-week trick: ask your team the same three questions twice — once in week one, once the day before the event. Same questions, same people, both times — that's what makes the two answers comparable.
Driver + guardrail. Speed bought by breaking things is not an elevation. Declare one driver metric you move and one guardrail you promise not to break: Higher Pace teams guard quality, Better Quality teams guard pace, Extended Value and A-Team-That-Thrives teams guard both. Judges love a team that knows what it's not allowed to sacrifice.
What a good metric looks like: measurable in ≤2 weeks at task scale · lives on the real project, not a mock · your agent plausibly moves it · baseline taken before the agent · direction declared. Not metrics: tokens spent, prompts written, number of agents, lines of code generated — that's the machinery, and the machinery is never the score. Pitch the growth, not the tool: "we elevated the team's happiness" beats "we used the tools a lot" every time.

📊No customer deployment? Projected is fine — no metric is not

Two weeks is short. Some teams won't get the agent in front of a customer by 7 Aug — that costs you nothing if your metric thinking is complete. The floor scorecard's universal scale is claim → demonstration → measurement; here's how your proof lands on it:

Proof levelWhat it looks like at the tableWhere it lands
MeasuredBaseline + after-number, same method, on the real project. "Release task: 4.5 h manual → 40 min with the agent, timed over 6 runs." top of the scale
ProjectedHonest baseline + a working pilot shown live + the projection math. "First-response baseline 4.1 h; the agent drafts in 6 min — projected ~70% cut, here's the pilot." demonstration — strong
ClaimedNo baseline, no number, adjectives only. "It makes us much faster." where teams lose

The rule in one line: the metric and the baseline are mandatory; the after-number may be measured or projected — but a projection without a shown pilot and visible math is just a claim wearing a suit.

🪧At your table on the night

The name plate carries your declared pillars — put the metric, direction and baseline right under them. Have ready: the before number · the after or projected number · one sentence on the method · at most one chart. When a judge says "run it now," the metric story should take 30 seconds and the demo the rest.

The 3-question team pulse

Ask each builder to score these 1–5 (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree); the average of the three is the pulse. Same questions, same people, week one and the day before the event.

  • Flow · "I can work on one thing without constant interruption."
  • Friction · "Waiting — on reviews, builds, environments, answers — rarely blocks me."
  • Cognitive load · "The effort my daily work takes feels reasonable for what it delivers."
