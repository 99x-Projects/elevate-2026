❓Questions we've actually been asked

Same questions kept coming up on the runway, so here they are in one place — from what this event even is, to what you need on the table on the night.

What this is
Is ELEVATE actually a hackathon?

Yes, in the ways that matter — one night, small teams, real building, a panel that picks winners at the end.

What's different is what "winning" means. A traditional hackathon rewards how much you could execute under time pressure — raw speed of execution. Agents have made execution speed cheap for everyone now; effort is no longer the scarce thing — judgment is. So ELEVATE doesn't score how fast you typed. It scores whether you gave an agent one real, recurring job on your real project, put a human decision gate in front of it, and can prove — with a number — that it moved the needle. Same night, same energy, a different scoreboard.

If not speed, what exactly gets scored?

Six steps, in order, on one card per team (the floor scorecard): the pain — is it real and costly · the mapping — pain → declared pillar → measure · the agent — human as operator or as approver · the gate — where the human decides, shown firing · the proof — did the declared pillar actually move · the offering — a named user, workflow and cost story beyond the demo. /30 total — see how the evening flows.

What counts as "genuinely agentic" — and what doesn't?

The qualifying bar, checked before a team is even ranked: genuinely agentic — a prompt chain you drive step by step is not an agent, it should be running multi-step work with you closer to approver than operator · ran on the real project — rough is fine, mock data is not · pillar declared — you're only scored on the pillar you name.

What you actually need to deliver
What do we need to bring to the table on the night?

One team, one metric — a declared pillar, one agent doing one recurring job end-to-end with a human gate in front of production, and two numbers: a baseline from before, and what the agent drove it to. Bring both, not one. The full playbook is on the Guide; for the concrete catalog of jobs teams elsewhere already hand to agents, see the Walkthrough.

Do we need a real customer deployment to score well?

No. Projected is fine — no metric is not. If production wasn't reachable in your two weeks, show the math behind your projection candidly. What costs a team is asserting movement with nothing behind it — not the absence of a live customer.

We don't know where to start — how do we pick our pain?

Walk your own project stage by stage and ask what recurs, what it costs, and who feels it — the four moves on the Guide. If that's still too abstract, the Walkthrough lays your project's lifecycle out left to right with ten spots teams elsewhere already hand to agents — steal the one that hurts your project most.

The night, and after
What's the timeline before and on the night?

Ramp-up Session 1 reaches every team as pre-recorded material (Fri 24 Jul — idea to a running start, to take in at your own pace), then event day, Fri 7 Aug, 5–9 PM: one floor, the panel discussion, judges' scoring lap 7:00–8:00, Judges' Pick 8 announced 8:15. Full detail on Event Calendar.

The eight teach the whole company what they built, the same evening. The strongest go on to the 99x expert panel — and from there, toward real customer offerings (that's exactly what scorecard step six, "the offering," is checking for). Every team leaves with 2–3 written notes from judges, Judges' Pick 8 or not.

3Pick your agent play

4One team, one metric

An elevation is a number that moved — and a number can only move against a baseline taken before the agent touched anything. The two weeks before event day are your measurement window.

Pick one metric. Baseline it in week one. Drive it in week two. Bring both numbers to your table.

Pick what your team can honestly measure in two weeks — SPACE for how the team works, HEART for what your users feel, DORA where you own the pipeline.

📐The metric menu — grouped to the NorthStar

One driver metric per team, from your declared pillar's column. Arrows show the direction that counts: ↓ drive it down, ↑ drive it up.

Higher Pace

SPACE efficiency & flow · DORA throughput
  • Lead time for a real task ↓ — time it before vs. with the agent, 3–6 real runs
  • Task cycle time (idea → in production) ↓
  • Deployment frequency ↑
  • Review / hand-off wait time ↓
  • Uninterrupted focus blocks per week ↑ (SPACE)
  • Perceived rate of delivery ↑ (DX Core 4 pulse)
Two-week trick: don't wait for org-level stats — stopwatch the actual slow task this week, then again with the agent next week.

Better Quality

DORA stability · rework thinking
  • Rework rate ↓ — DORA's newest key metric
  • Change failure rate ↓
  • Defects caught at the gate ↑ / escaped defects ↓
  • Failed-deployment recovery time ↓
  • Flaky-test rate ↓
Two-week trick: don't chase percentages that need months of data — count what actually happened: "our gate caught 9 of the 11 defects before they reached the merge."

Extended Value

Google HEART · customer KPIs · AI resolution
  • The KPI your customer already tracks ↑
  • Task success on the pilot ↑ — did the user finish the job? (HEART)
  • Ticket first-response / resolution time ↓
  • Resolution rate ↑ — share handled end-to-end by the agent, human at the gate
  • % effort moved from toil to billable work ↑
  • Cost per run / per transaction ↓
Two-week trick: pick the number the customer would put on their own dashboard — that's the one that survives the judges' Q&A.

A Team That Thrives

SPACE · DevEx · DX Core 4 effectiveness
  • Time reclaimed from toil, per person per week ↑
  • 3-question team pulse (friction · flow · cognitive load) ↑ — the questions are in the index
  • Interrupt & on-call load ↓
  • "Would you keep it on Monday?" ↑
Two-week trick: ask your team the same three questions twice — once in week one, once the day before the event. Same questions, same people, both times — that's what makes the two answers comparable.
Driver + guardrail. Speed bought by breaking things is not an elevation. Declare one driver metric you move and one guardrail you promise not to break: Higher Pace teams guard quality, Better Quality teams guard pace, Extended Value and A-Team-That-Thrives teams guard both. Judges love a team that knows what it's not allowed to sacrifice.
What a good metric looks like: measurable in ≤2 weeks at task scale · lives on the real project, not a mock · your agent plausibly moves it · baseline taken before the agent · direction declared. Not metrics: tokens spent, prompts written, number of agents, lines of code generated — that's the machinery, and the machinery is never the score. Pitch the growth, not the tool: "we elevated the team's happiness" beats "we used the tools a lot" every time.

📊No customer deployment? Projected is fine — no metric is not

No customer deployment by 7 Aug costs you nothing — if the metric thinking is complete. Proof lands on the scale claim → demonstration → measurement:

Proof levelWhat it looks like at the tableWhere it lands
MeasuredBaseline + after-number, same method, on the real project. "Release task: 4.5 h manual → 40 min with the agent, timed over 6 runs." top of the scale
ProjectedHonest baseline + a working pilot shown live + the projection math. "First-response baseline 4.1 h; the agent drafts in 6 min — projected ~70% cut, here's the pilot." demonstration — strong
ClaimedNo baseline, no number, adjectives only. "It makes us much faster." where teams lose

The rule in one line: the metric and the baseline are mandatory; the after-number may be measured or projected — but a projection without a shown pilot and visible math is just a claim wearing a suit.

🗺️Your project, left to right — and where an agent can own a job

Find where it compounds → delegate to an agent.

Say you're a forward-deployed engineer on one of our projects. Doesn't matter which role — every project runs the same line: someone states a requirement, a solution gets shaped, you build it, it gets tested, it ships, it runs, users react, and their feedback comes back around. That's the strip below, left to right.

Now walk your own project along that line — with an optimizer's eye. At each stage, ask what recurs and what it costs: the hours that go into grooming the backlog, re-running the regression suite, triaging the same class of ticket, walking the release checklist. Each of those is a recurring job with a measurable cost. And because the stages feed each other, the cost travels: time lost at requirements resurfaces as rework in construction, again in QA, again at release. That's what you're hunting — the stage where optimizing one job pays off everywhere downstream of it.

The ten numbered spots mark the common places — but you're not limited to them. Each is a job teams elsewhere already hand to an agent end-to-end, with a human still deciding what ships. They're a starting menu, not a boundary. If your project's biggest win sits somewhere else entirely — say a domain agent that carries your product's business rules, your customer's regulations, your data — that spot counts just as much. Walk the line, find where the optimization compounds hardest, and delegate that job to an agent.
